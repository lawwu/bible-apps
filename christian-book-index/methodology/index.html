Methodology

This index starts from award appearances, not from book metadata. Each public record — a prize, a category, a year, a status, a title, an author — is one row, and every row cites the public page it was taken from. Books are deduplicated out of those rows. The design (and much of the method) follows Benjamin Breen's Book Prize Index, adapted for the world of Christian publishing.

Principles

  • Every award appearance traces to a source URL: an official prize archive, an annual announcement page, or a public secondary archive when the primary page has vanished.
  • Language models help parse messy pages, but are never treated as a factual source. A row that cannot be traced to a fetched public page does not enter the catalog.
  • Coverage gaps are documented in the data rather than hidden by the interface. Prize archives are incomplete; the index is too, by design.
  • Corrections live in curation patch files and the catalog is regenerated — the published index is an output, never hand-edited authority.

The prize programs

Each program is tagged with the tradition it speaks from. That tag drives the index's distinctive feature: breadth— the count of distinct traditions that have honored a book. A book honored by both a Reformed jury and the Archbishop of Canterbury's panel carries a different kind of signal than one that swept awards within a single tribe. Rows marked ❧ in the catalog were recognized across two or more traditions.

Recognition score

The default ranking is a recognition score — a compact way to sort dense prize data, not a claim that one book is objectively better than another. Point values mirror the Book Prize Index:

All programs currently indexed count as major. A book that wins a category award and Book of the Year in the same year earns both — those are two distinct recognitions. Ties break by wins, then finalist appearances, then breadth, then earliest year.

Books and subjects

Award rows are deduplicated into books by normalized title (subtitles vary between sources) plus the first author's surname, with a patch file for the cases normalization gets wrong. Each book gets one subject from a small editorial shelf taxonomy — mapped from award category names, favoring winner-row categories — so the catalog doesn't double-count a book across shelves. Subjects are reader-facing shelves, not library classifications.

Limitations

  • Older archives thin out: the Gold Medallion era (1978–2005) and early Christianity Today years are only as complete as surviving public pages allow.
  • Programs differ in shape — some publish finalists, some only winners, some award every three years — so raw appearance counts are not comparable across programs.
  • The indexed programs skew Protestant and evangelical. Widening the bench of traditions (Catholic, Orthodox, academic religious studies) would make the breadth signal richer; that is a data problem, not a design problem.
  • Author names and titles appear as printed by each source; light normalization may retain variants.
