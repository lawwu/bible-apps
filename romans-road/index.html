The Romans Road

Around AD 57, a former enemy of the Christian movement named Paul wrote a long letter to the church in Rome — a city he had never visited. It contains the oldest sustained argument for the Christian message we have. Four sentences from it, read in order, have been used for centuries as the shortest honest summary of that message.

This page walks those sentences slowly. Under each one you'll find the threads — other passages, written across a thousand years, that say the same thing. They're there so you can check that nothing is being quoted out of context. Pull any of them.

I

The problem

For all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God;

Romans 3:23

The claim is not that everyone is as bad as they could be. It's that everyone — the cruel and the kind, the religious and the irreligious — falls short of the same standard: the glory of God. Measured against each other, we do fine. Measured against that, the letter says, the difference between us disappears.

The same thread, elsewhere

Ecclesiastes 7:20 · ~900 years earlier For there is not a just man upon earth, that doeth good, and sinneth not. Psalm 14:3 They are all gone aside… there is none that doeth good, no, not one. 1 John 1:8 If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us.
II

The stakes

For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord.

Romans 6:23

Notice the two economies in one sentence. Death is called a wage — something earned, paid out fairly. Eternal life is called a gift — something that can only be received, never invoiced. The whole letter turns on that asymmetry: the bad news is a payroll, the good news is a present.

The same thread, elsewhere

Ezekiel 18:20 · ~600 BC The soul that sinneth, it shall die. James 1:15 Sin, when it is finished, bringeth forth death. John 3:36 He that believeth on the Son hath everlasting life.
III

The proof of love

But God commendeth his love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us.

Romans 5:8

Every other rescue story waits for the drowning to reach for the rope. This one doesn't. The claim here is that God's move came first — while we were yet sinners — not after we cleaned up, apologized, or even wanted it. The cross, in this letter's argument, is not a demand for improvement. It's the receipt of a love that acted before it was loved back.

The same thread, elsewhere

John 3:16 · the most cross-referenced claim in the book For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life. John 15:13 · Jesus, the night before his death Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends. 1 Peter 3:18 Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust, that he might bring us to God.
IV

The response

That if thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God hath raised him from the dead, thou shalt be saved.

Romans 10:9

Notice what is not here: no ladder of merit, no probation period, no fine print. Say it plainly and mean it — that Jesus is Lord, and that the resurrection happened. A few lines later Paul quotes a prophet from eight centuries before him: “whosoever shall call upon the name of the LORD shall be saved” (Romans 10:13). The same sentence appears three times in the book, in three different ages — same door, never narrowed.

The same door, in three ages

Joel 2:32 · centuries before Christ Whosoever shall call on the name of the LORD shall be delivered. Acts 2:21 · Peter, seven weeks after the crucifixion Whosoever shall call on the name of the Lord shall be saved. Ephesians 2:8 For by grace are ye saved through faith… it is the gift of God.
V

The other side

Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ:

Romans 5:1

This is where the road comes out: not at a feeling but at a settled state — peace with God, the war over. The letter presses the point twice more: “there is therefore now no condemnation” (8:1), and nothing in death or life or anything else in creation “shall be able to separate us from the love of God” (8:38–39). Not a probation. A verdict.

The same thread, elsewhere

Isaiah 26:3 Thou wilt keep him in perfect peace, whose mind is stayed on thee. John 10:28 · Jesus I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand. 2 Corinthians 5:17 If any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away.

What now?

If you've walked this far and it rings true — or even if you only suspect it might — here is what people have done at the end of this road for two thousand years. None of it requires special words. All of it can happen today.

  1. Say it to God, plainly. Prayer is not a script; it's telling the truth to someone who is listening. In your own words: that you fall short, that you believe Jesus died and rose for you, that you're asking for the gift and not the wage. The words don't save — the One you're speaking to does.
  2. Read one Gospel, start to finish. Don't take a tract's word for who Jesus is — read an eyewitness account. John was written “that ye might believe”; Mark is the shortest. Any Bible, any translation, a few chapters an evening.
  3. Tell someone. The sentence you just read said “confess with thy mouth” — this was never meant to be a private transaction. Tell a Christian you know, or walk into a church that teaches the Bible and say you're new to this. They will remember their own first time down this road.

Still skeptical? Good — keep pulling threads. Every verse on this page opens in the cross-reference explorer, where you can see its full context and the hundreds of connections around it. This road was never afraid of being checked.
