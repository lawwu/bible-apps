Theological Triage

In 2005 Albert Mohler borrowed a word from the emergency room. Triage — from the French trier, to sort — is the discipline that sends a chest wound to surgery ahead of a scraped knee, and nobody calls that unfair. His argument: doctrine needs the same discipline. Some beliefs are the faith itself. Some order the shared life of a congregation. Some are worth a lifetime of friendly argument, and some were never legislated at all. A church that treats every question with equal alarm has not become more serious about truth — it has lost the ability to say which truths its life depends on.

Below is the ward as it is usually drawn — Mohler's three levels, plus the fourth that Marc Minter (and older theologians before him) kept for matters of conscience. Then the questions that decide a ranking, and a sorting room where you take the clipboard yourself.

The wardThe four levels

First order Immediate

“Is this Christianity at all?”

Doctrines the faith cannot lose and still be the faith: that Christ is fully God and fully man, that he bodily rose from the dead, that God is Trinity, that sinners are justified by faith alone, that Scripture speaks with God's own authority. These are the truths the ancient creeds were built to guard. To deny one is not to join a different denomination — it is to stand outside Christianity while perhaps still borrowing its words, the first move of every group the church has had to call a cult. These are the doctrines a follower of Christ must hold, the ones worth dying for.

  • Deity & humanity of Christ
  • Bodily resurrection
  • The Trinity
  • Justification by faith alone
  • Substitutionary atonement
  • Authority of Scripture

The line it draws: between Christian and non-Christian.

For I delivered unto you first of all that which I also received… 1 Corinthians 15:3

Paul's first of all — ἐν πρώτοις, “among the first things” — is the oldest triage decision on record.

Second order Urgent

“Can we be one congregation?”

Essential to the ordered life of a local church, but not essential to the gospel. A believer can be wrong here and still belong to Christ — yet a congregation cannot stay undecided, because practice forces the issue: this Sunday the church will baptize its infants or it will not; it will be governed by elders, bishops, or the gathered members; it will ordain women or it will not. So these are the doctrines that draw denominational lines. A Baptist and a Presbyterian can honor one another as brothers for a lifetime — and still cannot share a membership roll. Nearly every fork in the Denomination Tree is a second-order split.

  • Baptism — mode & subjects
  • Church government & eldership
  • The Lord's Supper
  • Predestination & election
  • Women's ordination †

† Filed here by Mohler — but see the Procrustean bed, below.

The line it draws: between congregation and congregation — not between believers.

…that thou mayest know how thou oughtest to behave thyself in the house of God… 1 Timothy 3:15
Third order Stable

“Can we differ in one pew?”

Doctrines serious Christians debate for a lifetime without threatening the fellowship of a single congregation. Most of eschatology lives on this shelf — the timetable of Christ's return, the nature of the millennium, the identity of the antichrist — along with the soul's state between death and resurrection, and whether reward and punishment come in degrees. Note where the triage line falls: that Christ will return is first-order and creedal; when, and in what sequence, is a conversation the church has been having since Patmos. Done well, the argument itself is a means of growth — iron sharpening iron across the potluck table.

  • Timing of Christ's return
  • The millennium
  • The antichrist
  • The intermediate state
  • Degrees of reward & punishment

The line it draws: between opinions — never between members. Same pew, different charts.

…and if in any thing ye be otherwise minded, God shall reveal even this unto you. Philippians 3:15
Fourth order No injury

“Did God legislate this at all?”

Mohler stopped at three. Marc Minter — with centuries of theologians before him — kept a fourth shelf for adiaphora, “indifferent things”: matters where Scripture supplies principles but no verdict. Where to school your children. Which job to take, which city to live in. Food and drink, music and media, dress. Here wisdom applies and commands do not exist, so the tag reads no injury and the discharge instructions are Romans 14: be fully persuaded in your own mind, and refuse to judge the one persuaded otherwise. To build a fence here — to bind another Christian's conscience where God left liberty — is not rigor. Minter names it legalism, full stop; grace and charity are the treatment plan.

  • Schooling
  • Vocation
  • Where to live
  • Food & drink
  • Music & media
  • Dress

The line it draws: none. Where God drew no line, neither may we.

…Let every man be fully persuaded in his own mind. Romans 14:5

Before you tag anythingThe nurse's three questions

How does a doctrine get its rank? Joe Rigney, writing from the trenches of actual church life, reduces the judgment to three questions:

  1. How clearly does Scripture speak?

    A doctrine cannot be ranked above its own clarity. The resurrection is stated in daylight prose; the millennium arrives wrapped in apocalyptic symbol. Rank accordingly.

  2. How close is it to the gospel?

    If this falls, does justification fall with it? Some doctrines are load-bearing walls; some are furniture. Push on each one and see what else moves.

  3. Does practice force a decision?

    A congregation must baptize somehow, be governed somehow, ordain someone. Whatever your private sympathies, a question the church cannot stay neutral on cannot be third order.

And one refinement for the second shelf: ask how the error behaves. Some errors stay where they are put; others, Scripture warns, will eat as doth a canker (2 Timothy 2:17) — they spread toward the foundations. Second order is a range, not a point: two doctrines can share a shelf and not share a seriousness.

Your shiftThe sorting room

Twelve cases are on the board. Read each chart, choose a tag, then compare your call with the textbook ranking — Mohler's, with Minter's fourth level, and the referees' notes where the textbook itself is contested. Keys 1–4 work too.

MalpracticeFour ways to fail the shift

i

Tag everything immediate

The fundamentalist reflex. Every scraped knee sent to surgery: third-order timetables treated as tests of fellowship, the church divided over charts and diagrams. Mohler names this the characteristic temptation of fundamentalism — wrongly dividing those God has joined.

ii

Tag nothing immediate

The liberal reflex. Chest wounds waved into the waiting room: first-order doctrine downgraded to private opinion until the ward holds nothing anyone would die for. Mohler's diagnosis of theological liberalism — ambiguity adopted as policy.

iii

Stretch the patient to fit the bed

The Procrustean reflex — named for the mythical innkeeper who made every guest fit his iron cot, stretching the short and trimming the tall. David Attebury's warning: a rank is a cooperation decision, not a measure of importance, and some second-order doctrines lean hard on first-order ground — creation, the authority of Scripture. File one as an ordinary tier-two dispute and you may have trimmed off what it was attached to. The bed must fit the patient.

iv

Fence the discharge lounge

The legalist reflex. Binding consciences on fourth-order matters — schooling, diet, dress — feels like seriousness and is its opposite. Dogmatized adiaphora, Minter writes, is “the very definition of legalism.” Where God gave liberty, the only malpractice is drawing lines.
